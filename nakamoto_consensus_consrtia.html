Nakamoto Consensus for Consortium Blockchains

I'm not sure that this is an accepted term of art yet, but here I use consortium blockchain to mean a distributed ledger shared among a consortium of companies. For example, a handful of banks may wish to keep a shared ledger for inter-bank transactions.

Hyperledger (and its Alternatives) are not Blockchain

Hyperledger calls itself a blockchain, but that's like calling a linked list a blockchain. Yes, technically it stores information in blocks which reference each other, but this is not a new idea. Remember that Nakamoto Consensus is the major technological development around here; Hyperledger does not use any new ideas whatsoever.

Let's take a trip back in time to the early 2000s. The e-Estonia movement (to digitize the government of Estonia) is in full swing, and X-Road is released for use. X-Road is essentially a data distribution platform with the following properties:

  • Any particular datum is stored only in the following locations:
    • On the computers of the appropriate governing body (e.g. a driver's license is stored in the Department of Transportation's computers, but not the Department of Education's)
    • In backup locations (Estonia currently backs up all its state records in Luxembourg. No joke!)
    • On the computers of any governmental body that requires the information (e.g. your driver"s license may be requested and stored on the copmuters of the police force)
  • Hundreds of government agencies and companies use X-Road as their database software
  • Separate instances of X-Road communicate over the internet. Each instance defines which data can be sent to whom, and under what circumstances.
  • All traffic is secured via cryptography, and all identities verified by digital signature

As evidenced by the Luxembourg backup, X-Road also facilitates shared databases between agencies (although it does require the agencies to configure their systems appropriately).

Let's also talk about server pools and load balancing. These ideas have been around for ages, and are another example of distributed database technology predating hyperledger. Esentially, a webapp will set up multiple servers which are all capable of offering the same service, and split its requests among all servers.

Basically, Hyperledger does have a valid use case, but it is not new technology, and in my opinion they are only calling it blockchain as a marketing gimmick.

Ok, we've listened to your rant, but can we get to the point?

Hyperledger can (by design) accommodate different consensus algorithms. Right now, most people use a Byzantine Fault-Tolerant (BFT) algorithm. However, most BFT derivatives can only work on fewer than ten nodes. So in a large network, around ten nodes will become a sort of central authority on which transactions get included and in what order.

The beauty of Nakamoto Consensus (currently synonymous with Proof-of-Work) is that it makes consensus among thousands of nodes possible. Is there some way to use it inside Hyperledger?

I think the answer is yes, and I talk about how that might be possible on this other rough page.
