The Proof-of-Work Arms Race

Allow me to say something controversial: Proof-of-Work is not inefficient. The usual argument against PoW goes something like this:

  • So much electricity is wasted. Hashing doesn't benefit anybody.
  • Blockchain isn't scalable because every transaction requires a lot of hashing work.

At least from an economic point of view, I don't think it's reasonable to say that the electricity used in PoW is wasted. Every miner who hashes, even if they don't produce a block, does contribute to the usefulness of the system. In some sense, that's the price paid for decentralization.

Another common argument is this: "Why not replace hashing with something more useful? And while we're at it, why not use the heat produced by the computational work to warm us up in the wintertime?" At first, this seems reasonable. But you see, changing the hashing to something more useful (as well as using the heat) simply serves to increase the revenue from mining. Thus, since your expected revenue is higher, you'll be willing to increase your mining power. This is the "marginal cost, marginal revenue" principle.

(EDIT: this doesn't paint the whole picture. Let's say that right now you're spending $100/day on mining, and making some amount of profit.Someone comes by and says that they'll sell you a rig that costs $50 a day to run. Should you buy it? Well, it depends; would your revenue increase by more than $50/day? If not, then you will be making less money, even if your total revenue is increased. In the case of doing useful work with PoW, you would only increase your hashing power as long as the expected increase in revenue is higher than your increase in costs. My argument is that you would still increase your hashing power if it had secondary uses)

The last misconception is to say that bitcoin is slow because of PoW. This is not really true. Actually, it's up to the blockchain's developer to decide on the block time (i.e. the expected amount of time between blocks). In bitcoin this was set to 10 minutes (I'm not aware of the reason) and in Ethereum it is 12 seconds. In Ethereum's case, this was about the lowest they could push it; if you let your block time get too short, then the network latency will cause a tremendous amount of forks. I talk about this is more detail on the throughput page.

"I still find it hard to believe that PoW isn't inefficient"

Well, I can't blame you for thinking that. It's a fact that it consumes a lot of energy, and likely a lot more than is theoretically required. And the title of this page should hint at what's coming next...

Here is the hypothesis: Nakamoto consensus is a very clever innovation in distributed databses which, among other things, makes cryptocurrencies possible. Proof-of-Work is necessary in Nakamoto consensus, but the "arms race" isn't!

The Game Theory Behind Arms Races/PoW

Most of this is directly copied from William Spaniel's excellent Game Theory 101 video. Consider two countries (A and B) that have an equal amount of weapons. Should either of the countries start spending on more weapons?

If country A buys weapons, and country B doesn't, then A has now gained an advantage and B is at a disadvantage. If both countries buy weapons, then both countries have essentially wasted money (but this is not as bad as letting the other country gain the upper hand). If neither country buys weapons, then everything stays the same. Let's organize this into a payoff matrix:

A/B
 Don't Buy
 Buy
Don't Buy
 0 / 0
 -2 / 2
Buy
 2 / -2
 -1 / -1

This situation is very reminiscent of buying hashing power. If you buy more, and your competition doesn't, then you gain an advantage over them. If nobody buys more, then nothing changes. If everyone buys more, then everyone is essentially wasting money!

It doesn't take long to notice that this is a classic Prisoner's Dilemma situation. All miners will continue to buy more hashing power despite very diminishing returns in revenue, since that is a dominant strategy. What we see here is an inefficient Nash equilibrium.

Is it Possible to Lower Bitcoin's Energy Usage?

Absolutely! All you have to do (it's really nothing) is get all miners on the planet to give up half of their hashing power. Easy! All miners would continue to earn the exact same revenue, but only pay half the electricity costs!

So clearly no miner would ever agree to sell off half of their hashing power. If they did, they would be more vulnerable to losing revenue in the future if e.g. more miners joined the network. Also, each miner thinks to themselves "I will make way more money if I'm the only one who doesn't cut their power in half". Game theory keeps coming back to bite us.

Right now I see two options that might be worth looking into.

  1. Coming up with some fancy PoW function that guarantees all miners have roughly equal power. I have no idea how this would work.
  2. In a permissioned system, you could simply count the number of blocks one miner is accumulating. Contrary to an arms race (where the only way to equalize would be for all other miners to match power) you could simply adjust the difficulty parameter for that one miner. There was a paper published about this recently, but I can't quite find it at the moment. Hopefully I'll remember to link it in in the near future.

One reason PoW works so well is because it is virtually impossible for one person to hold up the whole system (i.e. some kind of DoS attack). In PoS, PoA, Algorand, etc. there are cases where you can temporarily disrupt block creation by simply not participating. So if we found an automatically-adjusting PoW function, we would like it to maintain this robust liveness.
